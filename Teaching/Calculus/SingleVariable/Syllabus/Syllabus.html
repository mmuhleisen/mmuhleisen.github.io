Exposition

This is a complete course in single variable calculus. Traditionally, this subject is divided into two halves called Calculus I and Calculus II. At a high level, Calculus I is the mathematical study of change. The idea is to take a function f and come up with a set of tools which can be used to answer the following question.

Question

1

When the input of f changes from x0 to x1, what can be said about how much the output of f changes? In other words, what can be said about the difference f⁢(x1)−f⁢(x0)?

Exposition

The tools which answer this question revolve around an operation called “differentiation,” which is why Calculus I is also called differential calculus. In addition to its intrinsic usefulness in providing answers to the above question, applications also include estimating approximation errors, optimization, and understanding the effects of constraints.

Calculus II, again at a high level, is the mathematical study of size. It begins with the observation that the concept of area also plays a key role in answering the above question. By leveraging our ability to answer this question, we will learn not only a great deal about area, but also about length and volume. The expressions which represent areas, lengths, and volumes are called integrals, which is why Calculus II is also called integral calculus. In addition to its intrinsic usefulness, applications include finding probabilities, deciding on the convergence of certain infinite sums, and predicting future values of time-dependent quantities.

Warning

2

The official title of this course, “Calculus, Part I,” is misleading. That’s because at Penn, this course covers not only what is usually called Calculus I, but also Calculus II (see above). This has negatively impacted students in two ways.

  1. When other institutions see “Calculus, Part I” on a transcript, they may hesitate or refuse to give you credit for a course which also covers Calculus II. Please let me know if this concerns you; while I have written letters in the past to get these issues resolved, there is no guarantee it will work in every case.

  2. Students occasionally sign up for this class expecting only to cover Calculus I. When they discover they’re actually in a fast-paced Calculus I + II course, it has happened that it was already too late to switch to a different course. See the Prerequisites section for more information.

Your raw score will be determined from the components itemized below.

  • (0%) Homework.

    • For every topic on the syllabus, dozens of problems will be listed on Canvas, spread across various calculus textbooks as well as a variety of online resources. Homework will neither be collected nor graded.

    • You are absolutely not expected to do all the homework problems. There are deliberately far too many problems to solve in between classes, and the point is to stop when you feel confident about the material.

    • You are expected to find solutions to the homework problems you do work out either in the back of the textbook they were assigned from, online, or by any other means (see Technology Policy).

    • If you cannot reconcile your own solution to a problem with any solutions you look up, come talk to me about it. You will always have a chance to talk to me in between classes (see Office Hours).

    • Be sure to seek out the harder problems. If you attempt to solve them and fail, you’ll get a sense of what you still need to do to understand the material really well; if you succeed, it will boost your confidence considerably. Either way, easy problems will seem much easier. (You’ll also be better prepared for the quizzes, which tend to be hard.)

  • (25%) Syllabus Assessment

    • The purpose of the syllabus assessment is to prove to me that you’ve read this syllabus and understood how things are going to work.

      • The final deadline for the syllabus assessment is 5pm the day before the last day to add classes (see Registrar Dates). If you miss this deadline, you‘ll still have 24 hours to switch to a different course.

      • This year, unusually, the early and final deadlines are five hours apart.

    • Instructions

      • You will take the syllabus assessment on Canvas.

      • You may refer to the syllabus during the syllabus assessment, you will have unlimited attempts, you will have unlimited time, and if you’re Emperor Palpatine you will also have unlimited power.

  • (50%) Quizzes

    • Each quiz will emphasize the material covered in the previous class; at the same time, each quiz may be cumulative.

    • Timing

      • The pace of the course demands each class begin with a quiz.

      • There will not be a quiz on the first day of class (or on the day of the final).

    • Instructions

      • Each quiz is designed to take ten minutes, but you will have fifteen.

      • You will take the quizzes on Canvas during the first fifteen minutes of class.

      • You must be present in class with your camera on and microphone off while taking the quizzes.

      • With the exception of receiving live assistance from or working together with another person, you may use any means whatsoever to complete the quizzes. This includes permission to use computer algebra systems, search engines, and generative AI.

Exposition

In my experience grading for the Penn math department, it is rare that final grades are assigned according to the traditional rubric where letter grades are all spaced 10% apart. I believe the situation is similar at most top universities. In our class we will also abandon this tradition. Instead, I will determine your final grades using a curve.

When grades are curved, your score only has significance when compared to the average/median. This means a 50% on a quiz isn’t necessarily a good or a bad score, it all depends on how the rest of the class does. I will always report the statistics of a quiz when they are graded, and you should be able to see statistics on Canvas, too.

In most math classes I have graded for, the average and/or median corresponded roughly to a B+. This means if you do around average, you can assume your grade is somewhere in the range of high B to low A-. If you score above average by a healthy amount, you’re solidly in the A range; if you do worse, you’re solidly in the B range; and if you do significantly worse than the average, you’re at risk of getting a C or below. You will not receive letter grades on individual assignments, only one letter grade at the end of the class.

The exact way your letter grades are calculated is complicated, but it involves choosing ranges for each letter grade in such a way that the resulting distribution approximately matches grade distributions for the same class from previous years. This way, from the point of view of your final grades, you’re neither at an advantage nor at a disadvantage by having me as your instructor—even if you find my assessments difficult.

Exposition

Since homework will not be collected, you must not only choose the problems you will solve, but also, once you’ve done them, look up answers yourself to be sure your work is correct. To that end, several textbooks list solutions in an appendix or work certain problems out in an accompanying student solution manual; moreover, a great many websites contain repositories of calculus problems with solutions. You are encouraged to use any of these Resources, not just the few listed in next section (but you are not required to use any one in particular).

To some extent, I also encourage you to use Generative AI such as ChatGPT and Computer Algebra Systems such as Wolfram Alpha. If you’re stuck on a routine exercise, it means you’re missing something basic. In this situation, you might as well ask Generative AI to help you out—on the one hand because routine exercises are where Generative AI is the most reliable, and on the other hand because chances are the only thing you’re missing is something simple enough for the software to explain correctly. Do not ever use Generative AI for help on a difficult problem. You are supposed to get stuck on difficult problems, and the process of puzzling it out—however long it takes—is the undisputed, number one way to grapple with the concepts and learn what you’re actually doing. For now there is also the danger Generative AI will get difficult problems completely wrong, with or without you noticing. Computer Algebra Systems meanwhile have much higher accuracy, and are great for checking your work on a problem if you can’t find a solution somewhere. The downside is they don’t understand word problems, so they’re only useful once you’ve set up the equations.

Many but not all of the following resources are available for free. Regarding textbooks, remember you have access to the MPA library as well as other libraries at Penn. Let me know if you benefitted from a calculus resource I have not listed so I may recommend it to students in the future.

  • Some traditional Calculus textbooks (alphabetical by last name)

    • Calculus by Howard Anton, Irl Bivens, and Stephen Davis.

    • Calculus (Volume 1) by Tom Apostol.

    • Calculus and Its Applications by Larry Goldstein et al.

    • Thomas’ Calculus by Joel Hass et al.

    • Calculus: Single Variable by Deborah Hughes-Hallett et al.

    • First Course in Calculus by Serge Lang.

    • Calculus by Ron Larson and Bruce Edwards.

    • Calculus by Jon Rogawski, Colin Adams, and Robert Franzosa.

    • Calculus by Michael Spivak.

    • Calculus by James Stewart.

  • Learning Calculus online (no particular order)

    • Paul Dawkins’s Online Math Notes, which has a huge repository of both problems and solutions.

    • Gilbert Strang’s Calculus Online Textbook. Solutions are available chapter-by-chapter in the online instructor’s manual.

    • David Guichard’s online textbook.

    • Sigurd Angenent, an author of a calculus textbook used at the University of Wisconsin Madison, links to his book in the README file of its GitHub repository.

    • The works of our very own faculty at Penn.

      • Nakia Rimmer’s “Calculus II Workbook,” which contains over a hundred worked-out integral calculus problems, is available from the MPA library in DRL or can be purchased from his Calc Coach website.

      • Rimmer’s YouTube channel also has playlists on a variety of calculus topics.

      • Robert Ghrist’s YouTube channel contains the single variable calculus video textbook “Calculus Green.” As of this writing it remains a work in progress.

      • The Penn Online Learning YouTube channel hosts an earlier anthology of Ghrist’s single variable calculus videos.

    • The Fall 2006 Single Variable Calculus course taught by David Jerison at MIT on the OpenCourseWare site. If you look around the assignments and exams tabs, you’ll find many problems with accompanying solutions. I first learned calculus watching the recorded lectures available here!

    • Our syllabus overlaps considerably with the College Board’s BC Calculus curriculum, so you may consider making use of the corresponding Khan Academy course, which consists of videos and check-your-understanding type problems.

    • The quality of Wikipedia math articles typically ranges from good to excellent. While they often contain examples, they never contain practice problems.

    • H. Jerome Keisler’s textbook Elementary Calculus is remarkable for taking the concept of infinitesimal quantities very seriously. We will not take this approach in our class, but do take a look if you’re interested!

  • Calculus Software (no particular order)

    • Wolfram Alpha, a lite (but easier to use) version of Mathematica

    • Maple

    • Microsoft Math Solver

    • Symbolab

    • Regarding Generative AI, see the Technology Policy section above.

  • The resources on the department’s Calculus Help site.
