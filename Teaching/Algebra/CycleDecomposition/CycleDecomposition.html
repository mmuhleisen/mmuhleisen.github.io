Title

Permutations and Cycles

Definition

1

A permutation of a set X is a bijection X→X.

Proposition

2

The permutations of a set X are a group under composition. We write SX for this group, or sometimes Sn when X={1,2,…,n}.

Proof

Let X be a set, and let E be the set of all functions X→X. The composite of two functions X→X is also a function X→X, so composition is a binary operation on E. Function composition is associative, and the identity function is an identity element for composition. Since bijections are, by definition, precisely the invertible elements of E for composition, that SX=E× is a group follows from the first homework problem.

Definition

3

A subpermutation of a permutation σ:X→X is a permutation Y→Y obtained by restricting σ to a subset Y⊆X.

Remark

4

To give a subpermutation is to give a subset Y⊂X satisfying σ−1⁢(Y)=Y. Unpacking that condition says x∈Y iff σ⁢(x)∈Y.

Example

5

  1. There are always so-called “trivial” subpermutations corresponding to ∅⊆X and X⊆X.

  2. The set of even numbers is a nontrivial subpermutation of ℤ⁢⟶+2⁢ℤ. The set Y of positive even numbers is not a subpermutation because −2∈σ−1⁢(Y) while −2∉Y.

  3. Let X={1,2,3,4}. The subsets {1,3} and {2,4} are nontrivial subpermutations of the bijection X→X taking 1↦3, 2↦4, 3↦1, and 4↦2.

Definition

6

Let X be a finite set of cardinality (i.e. size) n, and let σ:X→X be a permutation. Then the following conditions on σ are equivalent, in which case σ is called a cycle (or a cyclic permutation).

  1. The only subpermutations of σ are ∅ and X.

  2. There exists some x∈X such that the map n→X taking k↦σk⁢(x) is a bijection.

  3. For all x∈X, the map n→X taking k↦σk⁢(x) is a bijection.

Proof

  • Since we assumed X is nonempty, 3 implies 2.

  • For 2 implies 1, claim that σn⁢(x)=x and prove this using n→X being a bijection. Now take a nonempty subpermutation σk⁢(x)∈Y⊆X. Since σ−1⁢(Y)=Y and σk⁢(x)∈Y, it follows σk+1⁢(x)∈Y. Repeating this argument shows σj⁢(x)∈Y for all j≥k. Finally, the claim implies every element of Y can be written as σj⁢(x) for some j≥k.

  • For 1 implies 3, take x∈X and consider the image of n→X. If the image is X you have a surjection between finite sets of the same size, hence a bijection. If not then σi⁢(x)=σj⁢(x) for some i≠j both less than n, hence σ|i−j|⁢(x)=x and since |i−j|<n the image is a nontrivial subpermutation (which contradicts the hypothesis).

Remark

7

Condition 1 says cycles are as small as possible. Condition 2 says X={x,σ⁢x,σ2⁢x,…,σn−1⁢x} for some x∈X. Condition 3 says that when you can write X in this form for some x∈X, you can write it in this form for any x∈X.

Definition

9

A cycle decomposition of a permutation σ:X→X is a partition of X into subpermutations all of which are cycles.

Theorem

10

Every permutation σ:X→X of a finite set X admits a unique cycle decomposition.

Proof

  • Uniqueness. Suppose there are two cycle decompositions of X. Choose x∈X, and let Y1 and Y2 be the unique parts of the two partitions containing x. Since Y1 and Y2 are subpermutations, σ−1⁢(Y1∩Y2)=σ−1⁢(Y1)∩σ−1⁢(Y2)=Y1∩Y2 shows Y1∩Y2 is a subpermutation of Y1 and Y2. But as Y1 and Y2 are cycles, and Y1∩Y2 contains x and is nonempty, Y1=Y1∩Y2=Y2. Since x was arbitrary, the two partitions have the same parts and are therefore equal.

  • Existence. By strong induction on the size of X. There is a unique way to partition a singleton set, and this partition is a cycle decomposition for the unique permutation of a singleton set. For the induction step, choose x∈X. Let Y be the image of the map ℤ→X taking k↦σk⁢(x). Then Y is a subpermutation as is easy to check; its complement is also a subpermutation because complements commute with preimages. If Y=X you have a cycle; otherwise, apply the induction hypothesis to Y and its complement.

Remark

11

There is no need to assume X is finite. We only defined cycles for finite sets, but condition 1 of the definition makes sense for sets of any size. With this definition, one can show the equivalence relation generated by x∼σ⁢(x) partitions X into ”cycles.” However, the natural language in which to express and to prove these statements is the language of group actions (to be covered later).
