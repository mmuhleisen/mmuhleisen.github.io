Title

The Euclidean Algorithm

Theorem

1

For any two distinct positive integers r0>r1>0, there is a unique positive integer n and a unique pair of sequences (qk)k=2n and (rk)k=0n of positive integers (whose elements are called quotients and remainders respectively) such that:

  1. The equation rk=rk+1⁢qk+2+rk+2 holds for all k≥0.

  2. The sequence (rk)k=0n of remainders is strictly decreasing.

  3. The last remainder rn divides the previous remainder rn−1.

Proof

Given rk>rk+1>0, the division algorithm uniquely determines a positive integer q and an integer rk+1>r≥0 such that rk=rk+1⁢q+r. If on the one hand r=0, then uniqueness implies there are no positive values of qk+2 and rk+2 satisfying the first two conditions in the theorem statement, so the sequences cannot continue and we must have n=k+1 in this case—and indeed if r=0 then rk+1 divides rk.

If on the other hand r>0, then rk+1 does not divide by rk, so the sequence must continue. Again by uniqueness in the division algorithm, we must define qk+2:=q and rk+2:=r as the next terms in the quotient and remainder sequences. Since there are no infinite decreasing sequences of positive integers, eventually this process must stop with some rn dividing rn−1.

Remark

3

The Euclidean algorithm efficiently determines the greatest common divisor of two integers r0 and r1, and therefore in particular whether r0 and r1 are relatively prime. Importantly, the algorithm determines the gcd without computing the prime factorizations of r0 and r1, a procedure for which the only (publicly?) known algorithms are comparatively very inefficient.

Proposition

4

With notation as in the Euclidean Algorithm, rn is the greatest common divisor of r0 and r1.

Proof

Remark

5

In the same way that long division of integers leads to the Euclidean algorithm mentioned above, long division of polynomials also leads to a Euclidean algorithm. In this case, you start with two polynomials r0 and r1 such that deg⁡r0>deg⁡r1, and by repeatedly performing long division obtain sequences of polynomials (qk)k=2n and (rk)k=0n such that the degrees of the rk form a strictly decreasing sequence until some rn divides rn−1. Again rn is the gcd of r0 and r1 by the same proof. The Euclidean algorithms for integers and for polynomials are simultaneously generalized in “Euclidean domains,” to be defined later.
