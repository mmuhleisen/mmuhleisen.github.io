Title

Notating Derivatives

Exposition

Let U=(a,b) be an open interval. If it exists, the derivative of a function f:U→ℝ is also a function U→ℝ (almost always different from f). When the derivative of a function f:U→ℝ exists, we say f is differentiable, and denote the derivative f⁢’.

Example

1

The derivative of the function f:ℝ→ℝ given by f⁢(x)=x2 is the function f⁢’:ℝ→ℝ given by f⁢’⁢(x)=2⁢x.

Exposition

When we agree to represent the elements of U with a specific variable, say x, then just as we use the notations f=f⁢(x) interchangeably, we also use all of the notations

f⁢’=f⁢’⁢(x)=d⁢fd⁢x=d⁢f⁢(x)d⁢x=dd⁢x⁢(f)=dd⁢x⁢(f⁢(x))

interchangeably. This has many advantages, which we will discuss when we interpret what the derivative means. For now, we highlight some disadvantages.

Warning

2

In the equation 2⁢cos2⁡(x)=1+cos⁡2⁢x of functions ℝ→ℝ, you can evaluate at x=π/8 and find that 2⁢cos2⁡(π/8)=1+1/2. Now in the previous example, we said dd⁢x⁢(x2)=2⁢x. Here too, you can evaluate at x=3 to obtain the equation dd⁢x⁢(x2)⁢(3)=6, and this is perfectly correct.

However, a common mistake is to evaluate at x=3 and conclude that dd⁢x⁢(9)=6. If you interpret 9 and 6 in this equation as constant functions, the statement is false because the derivative of a constant function is always the zero function. If you interpret either 9 or 6 as a number , the equation does not make sense—you only input functions into dd⁢x, and similarly the output of dd⁢x is always a function .

Warning

3

Say you have a function f:ℝ→ℝ, and for concreteness say f⁢(x)=x2. When anyone writes the expression f⁢(sin⁡(x)+1), it should always refer to the function (sin⁡(x)+1)2 and not to the function x2⁢(sin⁡(x)+1), which would be better denoted f⁢(x)⋅(sin⁡(x)+1).

Now consider the expression d⁢fd⁢x⁢(sin⁡(x)+1). If one follows the practice above, this expression should refer to 2⁢(sin⁡(x)+1) since d⁢fd⁢x=f⁢’ is a function. In practice, however, people often use this expression to refer to 2⁢x⁢(sin⁡(x)+1). Therefore, the expression is ambiguous! Readers are therefore encouraged never to open parentheses immediately after writing d⁢fd⁢x.

Instead, when one wants to unambiguously refer to the product of the derivative of f with sin⁡(x)+1, one should write

d⁢fd⁢x⋅(sin⁡(x)+1).

When one wants to unambiguously refer to the derivative of f evaluated at sin⁡(x), one can write either of the following:

d⁢fd⁢x|sin⁡(x)+1=d⁢fd⁢sin⁡(x).

All that being said, the expression dd⁢x⁢(sin⁡(x)), even though it looks very similar to d⁢fd⁢x⁢(sin⁡(x)), is completely unambiguous and refers to the derivative of sin⁡(x).

Exposition

Final comment: the notation f⁢’ for the derivative is named after the Italian/French mathematician/astronomer Joseph-Louis Lagrange while the notation d⁢fd⁢x is named after the German philosopher/mathematician Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. Isaac Newton’s notation f˙=f˙⁢(t) is only common when the letter t is used for the independent variable.
