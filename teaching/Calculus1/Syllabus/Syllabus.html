Course Description

Exposition

Functions may be considered the core object of study in mathematics. From finding the zeros of linear and quadratic polynomials to establishing trig identities and log rules, functions already play a significant role in precalculus mathematics education. To understand a function at this level is to know the relationship between input and output values, allowing one to solve the quintessential precalculus problem: find x if f⁢(x)=0 and f is some complicated function. The precalculus guiding principle is therefore to study functions individually, by how they act on numbers.

The guiding principle in calculus is to study functions in relation to each other. For example, our first unit will be differentiation, a procedure which transforms functions into other functions. The quintessential calculus problem is correspondingly to find f, a function, if D⁢(f)=0 and D is some procedure involving the derivative. Equations like these are called differential equations, and they are good at describing everything from the economy to elementary particles. Two techniques for solving differential equations that we’ll cover in this class are called integration and Taylor series. Along the way we’ll find many other uses for these concepts, from measuring sizes (i.e. lengths, areas, volumes) to controlling errors in approximations.

Grading

Exposition

In order to pass the course, you must receive a perfect score on the syllabus quiz, which you will take on canvas and submit before the second class. You may refer to the syllabus during the quiz and you will have unlimited attempts. The purpose of the syllabus quiz is to prove to me that you’ve read and understood all the administrative details in this syllabus. Once you’ve passed the syllabus quiz, your raw grade will be determined from the following components, and then curved upward if necessary to match grade distributions from recent summer iterations of MATH 1400.

  • (0%) Homework. For every topic on the syllabus, dozens if not hundreds of problems will be listed on Canvas, spread across various calculus textbooks as well as a variety of online resources. Homework will neither be collected nor graded. Please take note of the following.

    • You are absolutely not expected to do all the homework problems. There are deliberately far too many problems to solve in between classes, and the point is to stop when you feel confident about the material.

    • You are expected to find solutions to the homework problems you do work out either online, in the back of the textbook they were assigned from, or by any other means (see Resources ).

    • If you cannot reconcile your own solution to a problem with any solutions you look up, come talk to me about it. You will always have a chance to talk to me in between classes (see Office Hours ).

  • (20%) Final Exam. There will be a traditional final exam during the last class.

    • The final is designed to take 1.5 hours, but you will have 2.5 hours.

    • The final is cumulative.

    • You may use any printed resource to complete the final, for instance your class notes, a “cheat sheet,” or indeed any textbook(s) of your choice.

    • You may not use any electronic device to complete the final unless pre-approved.

    • Unless you have a pre-approved arrangement, not being present at the final will result in a grade of zero. No exceptions will be made.

    • Collaboration on the final exam is forbidden.

  • (80%) Quizzes. The pace of the course demands each class begin with a quiz. Please take note of the following.

    • There will not be a quiz on the first day of class (or on the day of the final).

    • There will be a quiz on review day (8/7/24) emphasizing the material covered in the previous class (8/5/24).

    • Each quiz is designed to take five minutes, but you will have fifteen.

    • Each quiz will emphasize the material covered in the previous class; at the same time, each quiz will be cumulative.

    • You may use any printed resource to complete quizzes, for instance your class notes, a “cheat sheet,” or indeed any textbook(s) of your choice.

    • You may not use any electronic device to complete quizzes unless pre-approved.

    • Unless you have a pre-approved arrangement, not being present at a quiz will result in a grade of zero. No exceptions will be made.

    • Collaboration on quizzes is forbidden.

Resources

Exposition

Since homework will not be collected, you must not only solve assigned problems but also look up solutions yourself to be sure your work is correct. To that end, several textbooks list answers in an appendix or work certain problems out in an accompanying student solution manual; moreover, a great many websites contain repositories of calculus problems with solutions, and a great deal of software has been written to solve just about any calculus problem. You are encouraged but not required to use any of these resources, not just the few listed below.

For our course, the main purpose of these resources is to serve as repositories of problems and solutions rather than as pedagogical material. While I will teach you calculus in a slightly different (and hopefully better) way than all of the resources listed below, the problems I will test you on will be just like the problems in these resources, namely, standard calculus problems. I nevertheless encourage you to seek out more and more perspectives on calculus if you find yourself stuck on a concept. Each resource explains each concept a little differently—and if you don’t find one that clicks, come talk to me and we’ll figure it out.

You may find generative AI and calculus-specific software very useful for solving math problems, but I seriously discourage you from coming to rely on them. On the one hand, the problem with generative AI is that it may output complete nonsense—even if beautifully formatted and boldly asserted. Worse, it will drivel about even on simple calculus queries. You simply cannot (yet?) trust ChatGPT to get word problems right with any kind of consistency. On the other hand, the problem with most calculus-specific software is its limitation to evaluating expressions when the essential step in many problems is to come up with the correct expression to evaluate in the first place.

Many but not all of the following resources are available for free. Regarding textbooks, remember you have access to the MPA library located in DRL 3N1 as well as other libraries at Penn. Any edition of a textbook will do: just email me what edition you’re using and I will add problems from that edition to future assignments. Note also that some textbooks have ”Early Transcendental” stunt doubles. For the purposes of our course, this is an irrelevant distinction—the versions both with and without some variant of the phrase “Early Transcendental” in the title will do perfectly well. Finally, do let me know if you benefitted from a calculus resource I have not listed so I may recommend it to students in the future.

  • Some Traditional Calculus Textbooks (alphabetical by last name)

    • Calculus (Volume 1) by Tom Apostol. Most recent edition: 2nd, 1991.

    • Calculus and Its Applications by Larry Goldstein et al. Most recent edition: 15th, 2022.

    • Calculus: An Integrated Approach to Functions and Their Rates of Change (Volume 2) by Robin Gottlieb. Most recent edition: 1st, 2018.

    • Thomas’ Calculus by Joel Hass et al. Most recent edition: 15th, 2022.

    • Calculus: Single Variable by Deborah Hughes-Hallett et al. Most recent edition: 8th, 2020.

    • First Course in Calculus by Serge Lang. Most recent edition: 5th, 1986.

    • Calculus by Ron Larson and Bruce Edwards. Most recent edition: 12th, 2022.

    • Calculus by Jon Rogawski, Colin Adams, and Robert Franzosa. Most recent edition: 4th, 2019.

    • Calculus by Michael Spivak. Most recent edition: 4th, 2008.

    • Calculus by James Stewart. Most recent edition: 9th, 2020.

  • Learning Calculus Online (no particular order)

    • Paul Dawkins’s Online Math Notes , which has a huge repository of both problems and solutions .

    • Gilbert Strang’s Calculus Online Textbook . Solutions are available chapter-by-chapter in the online instructor’s manual .

    • The Fall 2006 Single Variable Calculus course taught by David Jerison at MIT on the OpenCourseWare site. If you look around the assignments and exams tabs, you’ll find many problems with accompanying solutions. I first learned calculus watching the recorded lectures available here!

    • The works of our very own faculty at Penn.

      • Nakia Rimmer’s “Calculus II Workbook,” which contains over a hundred worked-out integral calculus problems, is available from the MPA library in DRL or can be purchased from his Calc Coach website.

      • Robert Ghrist’s YouTube channel contains the single variable calculus video textbook “Calculus Green.” As of this writing it is a work in progress.

    • David Guichard’s online textbook . Solutions are only given for select exercises.

    • Sigurd Angenent, an author of a calculus textbook used at the University of Wisconsin Madison, links to his book in the README file of its GitHub repository . Solutions are only given for select exercises.

    • Our syllabus overlaps considerably with the College Board’s BC Calculus curriculum, so you may consider making use of the corresponding Khan Academy course , which consists of videos and check-your-understanding type problems.

    • The quality of Wikipedia math articles typically ranges from good to excellent. While they often contain examples, they never contain practice problems.

    • H. Jerome Keisler’s textbook Elementary Calculus is remarkable for taking the concept of infinitesimal quantities very seriously. We will not take this approach in our class, but do take a look if you’re interested!

  • Calculus Software (no particular order)

    • Wolfram Alpha , a lite (but easier to use) version of Mathematica .

    • Maple

    • Microsoft Math Solver

    • Symbolab

    • I removed ChatGPT and other AI software from this list because I was unimpressed by their unreliable performance in experiments.

  • The resources on the department’s Calculus Help site.

I was not compensated in any way for listing any of the resources above.
